The Ultimate Revelation Of Russian Trolls


Trolls
 Cassy Hayes and Jasmine Coleman were among the first fans to arrive outside the Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center in Los Angeles where Michael Jackson was brought and later pronounced dead. How had Hayes, 25, and Coleman, 21, heard the news so quickly?

Twitter.

The two young women had learned about Jackson's health like so many who get their news nowadays: by reading the ever-flowing feed of real-time information on the microblogging service.

Jackson's unexpected death at 50 was just one of the many news event where Twitter played a central role. But just as quickly as Twitter has emerged as a news source, so, too, has its susceptibility to false rumors and false news become abundantly apparent.

Twitter’s constantly updating record of up-to-the-minute reaction has in some instances threatened to usurp media coverage of breaking news. It has also helped many celebrities, athletes and politicians bypass the media to get their message directly to their audience. These days it is no surprise to see politicians in the US converse with each other through the medium. And as our eyes are turned to Twitter, so has our way of collecting information about the world. There is something about the short, quick messages (of a maximum of 280 characters) that fits within our way of life at the moment: it has to be short, but especially quick.

In January, Twitter emailed nearly 700,000 users to tell them they may have engaged with the Russian accounts. Less than two weeks later, Twitter announced the number of users notified had more than doubled. These were Twitter users set-up from Russia and engaging in inflammatory tweets, with the goal of disrupting and dividing a nation’s public, and thus their political policies.

Thankfully, at the request of NBC News, three sources familiar with Twitter's data systems cross-referenced the list of names released by Congress, excluding any account that Twitter later restored, to create a partial database of tweets that could be recovered from the suspended accounts.

I took a deeper look in to this database, to see if I saw something unusual. There exists this idea that Russian trolls on Twitter engaged in the spread of fake news and support, with the aim of influencing an election, in particular the 2016 United States Presidential Elections, which was won by Donald Trump in November. How much of this is actually true?

I started looking for some evidence to support this claim, and looked at how many tweets were actually sent out by these trolls. I displayed the data in the (interactive) chart at the bottom of this page. Do you also notice something… particular?
